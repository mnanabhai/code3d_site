The Code3D web application is a programmatic 3D modelling and printing simulation platform for increasing student motivation in introductory programming courses.

The problem.

Computer programming is becoming increasingly relevant in a world of rapid digitisation. A global acknowledgement of the importance of computer science is growing. However, the growth of individuals with computing skills has not kept up to pace with the growth of technology, creating a large skills gap, especially in developing countries such as South Africa.

Learning to program is not easy. The computer science major has some of the highest drop-out rates across all disciplines â€“ it is estimated that fewer than 40% of students enrolling for a computer science major persist to the completion of their degree.

Our solution.

Graphics libraries such as p5.js, Processing and Logo Turtle were created to motivate new programmers, which made drawing and creating program visualizations easy and efficient. Extending this principle, an initial tool was proposed, that allowed students to create and print 3D artefacts effortlessly. By bridging programming and 3D printing, students have the possibility to explore emerging technologies like 3D printing, which is a very promising and exciting technology for both industry and education.

However, due to constraints imposed by the COVID-19 pandemic, the Code3D tool employs 3D printing simulation rather than physical printing.

Project aims and hypotheses

We aim to investigate the effectiveness of this approach as a tool for teaching introductory programming - in particular, its effects on improving students' motivation and engagement with the subject. We do this by evaluating the Code3D tool among computer science educators.

Our hypotheses are as follows:

  1. Educators will consider Code3D user-friendly for novices
  2. Educators will consider Code3D useful for increasing student motivation in learning introductory programming

Research Methodology

Due to the constraints imposed by the pandemic, it was chosen to use expert evaluation for assessing the system. On account of the small available sample size, a mixed methods approach was employed, combining both quantitative and qualitative methods. This included surveys regarding system usability and motivation, as well as interviews with participants to discuss in greater depth their experience using the system.
